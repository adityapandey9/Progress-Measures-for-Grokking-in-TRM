\section{Nanda calibration}

The one-layer Nanda-style MLP baseline (fidelity~A) validates our progress-measure implementation against \citet{nanda2023progress}.

Multi-seed aggregates (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}): test accuracy $\HybridNandaAcc$, final-checkpoint faithful FVE $\HybridNandaFVE$ under a fixed five-frequency reconstruction.
Best-FVE checkpoints reach higher FVE (Table~\ref{tab:checkpoint_compare}).
Figure~\ref{fig:hybrid_comparison} places calibration on the model ladder.

\paragraph{Key-frequency count must be data-driven.}
\citet{nanda2023progress} set the number of key frequencies from the number of
distinct frequencies the MLP neurons specialize in; it is not a fixed constant.
Truncating the trigonometric reconstruction at exactly five frequencies
under-reports models whose circuit spans six to nine frequencies. When we instead
select frequencies until the bias-corrected FVE plateaus (Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_methods}),
all $\HybridNandaSeedsClean$/$\BMINSeeds$ Nanda seeds reach faithful trig-FVE
$\geq 0.95$ (mean $\HybridNandaFVEAdaptive$, $K=\NandaAdaptiveKRange$ frequencies),
matching the $\sim$95\% reported by \citet{nanda2023progress} and confirming our
progress-measure stack is correctly calibrated.
The five-frequency value is therefore a lower bound, not a ceiling: the apparent
calibration shortfall is a metric artifact, not a defect of the model or training.
Figure~\ref{fig:fve_calibration} shows FVE rising to $\approx 0.99$ as $K$ grows
for the Nanda baseline (and for the two converged minimal-TRM seeds), while
degraded seeds and full TRM plateau far below.
Table~\ref{tab:corrected_fve} summarizes legacy vs.\ adaptive FVE per model.
